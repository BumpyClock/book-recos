## Sci-Fi Series to Binge

### The Stormlight Archive by Brandon Sanderson
- **Series?** 5 books (of planned 10), each 1000+ pages. Series binger's dream.
- **Why for you:** Epic adult fantasy with deep world-building, intricate magic systems, and genuine character growth. No YA. Characters deal with depression, trauma, and leadership.
- **Award/recognition:** Hugo finalist multiple times
- **Vibe comp:** The ambition and scope of Game of Thrones with the world-building obsession of the Expanse
- **Heads up:** Very long. Sanderson's prose is functional, not literary. But the characters and world are incredible.

### The First Law Trilogy by Joe Abercrombie
- **Series?** 3 books + 3 standalones + a second trilogy (9 books total in the world)
- **Why for you:** Grimdark fantasy with razor-sharp wit and deeply flawed, unforgettable characters. Subverts every fantasy trope. Hilarious narrative voice.
- **Award/recognition:** Locus Award finalist, British Fantasy Award
- **Vibe comp:** Game of Thrones' moral complexity meets Murderbot's sardonic inner monologue
- **Heads up:** Very dark. Violence. Cynical. But genuinely funny.

### The Mimicking of Known Successes by Malka Older
- **Series?** Book 1 of 3 (Mossa & Pleiti series)
- **Why for you:** Mystery + sci-fi mashup set on Jupiter. A Holmes-and-Watson-inspired detective duo. Short, witty, atmospheric. Exactly your Tainted Cup sweet spot.
- **Award/recognition:** Hugo finalist for Best Novella
- **Vibe comp:** Tainted Cup's mystery-in-a-speculative-world, but cozier and shorter
- **Heads up:** Novella-length (~170 pages). Quick read.

### Someone You Can Build a Nest In by John Wiswell
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** A shapeshifting monster falls in love with a human. It's a romance-horror-comedy with a protagonist who has a genuinely alien perspective but is deeply endearing. Witty voice.
- **Award/recognition:** Nebula Award winner (2025)
- **Vibe comp:** Murderbot's "I'm not human but I have feelings" energy meets Wayfarers' warmth, wrapped in a horror-comedy
- **Heads up:** Body horror elements played for both laughs and emotion

### Starter Villain by John Scalzi
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** You read Redshirts. This is Scalzi at his funniest — a man inherits his uncle's supervillain business. Extremely witty. Fast read.
- **Vibe comp:** Redshirts' humor and self-awareness, but about corporate villainy instead of Star Trek
- **Heads up:** Light. Not deep. Pure fun.

### The Goblin Emperor by Katherine Addison
- **Series?** Standalone (with a companion novel)
- **Why for you:** A half-goblin becomes emperor after his family is assassinated. Character-driven, warm, political intrigue with a genuinely kind protagonist. Court mystery + fantasy.
- **Award/recognition:** Hugo, Nebula, and Locus finalist
- **Vibe comp:** The political intrigue of Game of Thrones filtered through Wayfarers' warmth and decency
- **Heads up:** Lots of invented names to track. Slow-burn.

## Literary Fiction

### Tomb of Sand by Geetanjali Shree (translated by Daisy Rockwell)
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** First Hindi-language novel to win the International Booker Prize. An 80-year-old Indian woman confronts Partition, gender, and borders. Multi-generational, sweeping, and funny.
- **Award/recognition:** International Booker Prize (2022)
- **Vibe comp:** Covenant of Water's multi-generational sweep + the satirical voice you love, rooted in Indian history
- **Heads up:** Long (725 pages). Experimental style. Deliberately playful with language.

### Vera, or Faith by Gary Shteyngart
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** New Shteyngart! Through the eyes of a precocious 10-year-old in near-future America. Same wit, tenderness, and biting satire as Super Sad True Love Story.
- **Vibe comp:** More from the author of your possibly-favorite-book-ever
- **Heads up:** Published 2025. Near-future satire again.

### Fire Bird by Perumal Murugan (translated by Janani Kannan)
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** JCB Prize winner (2023). Tamil literature in translation. A rooster's-eye-view of village life in South India. Surprising, lyrical, and unlike anything else on your list.
- **Award/recognition:** JCB Prize for Literature (2023)
- **Vibe comp:** A completely different lens on Indian life than anything you've read
- **Heads up:** Translated from Tamil. Very different from your usual reads — in a good way.

### Yellowface by R.F. Kuang
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** From the Babel author. A white woman steals her dead Asian friend's manuscript and publishes it as her own. Satirical, compulsively readable, skewers publishing, race, and cancel culture. Exactly the satirical literary fiction you love.
- **Vibe comp:** Shteyngart's satirical intelligence applied to the publishing world. Dark, funny, uncomfortable.
- **Heads up:** Literary thriller, not speculative. Very fast read.

### Trust by Hernan Diaz
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** Four interlocking narratives about a wealthy couple in 1920s New York — each one rewriting the "truth." Formally brilliant. Satirizes wealth, narrative control, and capitalism.
- **Award/recognition:** Pulitzer Prize for Fiction (2023)
- **Vibe comp:** If you loved the dual-format storytelling of Super Sad True Love Story, this does something similar but with four unreliable perspectives
- **Heads up:** Literary puzzle box. Rewards patience.

### The Loneliness of Sonia and Sunny by Kiran Desai
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** Indian diaspora author (won the Booker in 2006 for The Inheritance of Loss). New novel on the 2025 Booker shortlist. South Asian identity, immigrant experience.
- **Award/recognition:** 2025 Booker Prize shortlist
- **Vibe comp:** Covenant of Water's sweeping ambition through a diasporic lens
- **Heads up:** Just published 2025.

## More Sci-Fi Worth Exploring

### A Memory Called Empire by Arkady Martine
- **Series?** Book 1 of 2 (Teixcalaan duology, complete)
- **Why for you:** Political space opera about identity, empire, and belonging. An ambassador arrives at a galactic empire's capital and must navigate a murder mystery while being culturally absorbed. Hugo winner.
- **Award/recognition:** Hugo Award winner for Best Novel (2020)
- **Vibe comp:** The Expanse's political intrigue + Ministry of Time's identity/belonging themes
- **Heads up:** Dense world-building with invented naming conventions. Push through the first 50 pages.

### The Mountain in the Sea by Ray Nayler
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** What if octopuses developed intelligence and language? A marine biologist tries to make first contact. Big ideas about consciousness, AI, and what it means to be sentient.
- **Award/recognition:** Nebula finalist, Locus finalist, Arthur C. Clarke finalist
- **Vibe comp:** Three-Body Problem's philosophical ambition meets Wayfarers' empathy for non-human minds
- **Heads up:** Contemplative. Multiple POVs. Ideas-driven.

### Nettle & Bone by T. Kingfisher
- **Series?** Standalone
- **Why for you:** A princess must kill a prince to save her sister. Dark fairy tale with a genuinely funny, self-deprecating narrator. T. Kingfisher dominates recent Hugo/Nebula lists for good reason.
- **Award/recognition:** Hugo Award winner for Best Novel (2023), Nebula finalist
- **Vibe comp:** Game of Thrones' dark stakes told with Murderbot's wry inner monologue
- **Heads up:** Relatively short (~250 pages). Quick, satisfying read.
